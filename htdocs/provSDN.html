SURIGAO DEL NORTE

Local dishes, ingredients, and food stories from Surigao del Norte

Overview

Surigao del Norte, gateway to the Philippine Deep and home to the world-famous Siargao Island, is a province shaped by the ocean. Its scattered islands, turquoise shallows and strong Pacific winds influence everything from daily routines to local cooking styles. Fishing villages line the coasts, and island communities maintain traditions that revolve around tides, reefs and the rich marine life surrounding them.

The provincial capital, Surigao City, serves as the region’s central hub — a place where boats, seafood, trade, and travelers constantly intersect. Markets here brim with fresh catch: reef fish, crabs, squid, seaweed and shellfish that arrive from nearby islands. This deep connection to the sea gives Surigao del Norte a vibrant food culture, one that celebrates freshness, acidity and fire-kissed flavors.

Culturally, Surigao del Norte blends heritage from indigenous Mamanwa communities, long-standing coastal groups, and Visayan settlers. Island festivals highlight maritime traditions, boat-building, and distinct Surigaonon dances. The province’s laid-back yet sea-driven lifestyle shows in its cuisine — simple ingredients elevated by technique, freshness and centuries of living beside the Pacific.

Notable Foods

Surigao del Norte’s cuisine is coastal at its core — sour, smoky, fresh and bright, often spotlighting what was caught only hours earlier.

- Kinilaw (Surigaonon-style ceviche): Widely considered one of the province’s signature preparations. Surigaonon kinilaw traditionally uses fresh fish or shellfish “cooked” in vinegar, calamansi and tabon-tabon fruit, which gives the dish its clean, slightly creamy, distinctly local flavor.

- Grilled fish and seafood platters: With coastal and island communities everywhere, grilling is a default cooking style. Reef fish, squid, prawns and crabs are often simply seasoned and fire-grilled — dishes that capture the province’s preference for honest, unmasked flavors.

- Sayongsong: A unique Surigaonon rice delicacy made from steamed, ground rice and coconut milk, wrapped in cone-shaped banana leaves. Its lightly sweet, soft texture makes it a favorite snack or pasalubong.

- Seaweed dishes and lato salads: Fresh seaweeds, especially lato, appear in salads with tomatoes, onions and a citrus-vinegar dressing. These bright, refreshing sides are staples of coastal dining and reflect the province’s reliance on marine harvests.
