SURIGAO DEL SUR

Local dishes, ingredients, and food stories from Surigao del Sur

Overview

Surigao del Sur stretches along Mindanao’s eastern coast, facing the open waters of the Pacific. The province is defined by dramatic shorelines, river systems, and forested mountains that feed into fertile lowlands. This meeting of sea and hinterland gives the area a rich culinary identity — one shaped by both coastal abundance and upland traditions.

Communities here are deeply connected to the water: fishing towns thrive along the coast, while inland municipalities rely on rivers and fertile soils for crops like rice, coconut and root vegetables. Surigao del Sur’s economy is rooted in these everyday harvests, and its cuisine reflects this balance — fresh seafood, hearty stews, and dishes that rely on straightforward ingredients elevated by local techniques.

Culturally, the province is home to various indigenous groups, including the Manobo and Mamanwa peoples, whose traditions remain visible in festivals, rituals and craftwork. Surigaonons take pride in their hospitality, their coastal rhythms and their distinct food culture, which blends local practices with influences from neighboring Surigao del Norte, Agusan provinces and Visayan settlers.

Notable Foods

Surigao del Sur’s food is soulful, coastal and comforting — a mix of seafood-forward dishes and hearty rural staples.

- Tinolang Isda (fish in ginger broth): A comforting staple across the province, often made with fresh reef fish, malunggay leaves and vegetables. Its warm, clear broth highlights the freshness of local seafood.

- Seafood kinilaw: Similar to Surigao del Norte but with its own regional twists. Fish or shellfish are cured in vinegar and citrus, mixed with onions, chili and occasionally coconut. The result is a bright, refreshing dish that appears in both households and seaside eateries.

- Sayongsong variants: While famous in Surigao del Norte, Surigao del Sur also enjoys its own take on this cone-wrapped rice delicacy. Soft, lightly sweet and coconut-rich, it is a beloved festival and merienda treat.

- Inato-style grilled dishes: In many towns, simple charcoal grilling (“inato-style”) is popular — from chicken and pork to fish and squid. Basting sauces vary by municipality, but the smoky, straightforward preparation is a local favorite.
