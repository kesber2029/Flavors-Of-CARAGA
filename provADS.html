AGUSAN DEL SUR

Local dishes, ingredients, and food stories from Agusan del Sur

Overview

Lying in the heart of CARAGA, Agusan del Sur is a province of wide rivers, lowland marshes and deep forest — a place where food and culture are shaped by water, harvest cycles and long-standing indigenous traditions. The great Agusan River threads the province from north to south and feeds the Agusan Marsh, the largest wetland in the Philippines; these waterways are more than landmarks here, they are lifeblood — transporting people and produce, shaping local recipes, and anchoring community stories.

This landscape gives Agusan del Sur a reputation as a quietly resilient agricultural heartland. Rice and corn dominate the fields, while banana and coconut groves edge the highways; fishing the river and lakes supplies the fresh, humble proteins that appear at nearly every kitchen table. The province’s economy and foodways remain closely tied to the seasons and to small-scale producers — farmers, fishermen, and indigenous harvesters who continue practices passed down through generations.

Culturally, Agusan del Sur is a mosaic. Several indigenous peoples — including Manobo and Higaonon groups — maintain visible presence and influence in the province, keeping songs, craft traditions and rice-field rituals alive even as migrant communities from the Visayas and Luzon have changed the demographic mix. Festivals and community events often highlight indigenous dances, storytelling and forest-to-table food traditions, giving visitors a chance to see how heritage and everyday life overlap.

Notable Foods

What you’ll taste in Agusan del Sur is straightforward, seasonal and built around what the land and river provide.

- Sinuglaw (smoke + cure): A regional favorite described as a pleasing marriage of two techniques — grilled or smoky elements (“sinugba”) paired with raw cured components (“kinilaw”). Sinuglaw celebrates freshness and contrast: charred meat or fish paired with bright, vinegar- or citrus-based dressings. It’s one of the province’s dishes that speaks to both river harvests and coastal influences.

- River fish dishes (tinolang isda, grilled tilapia, stews): Freshwater fish such as tilapia, mudfish and catfish appear in comforting broths and simple grilled preparations. Tinolang-style fish soups — light, gingered broths with local greens — are typical comfort food, emphasizing the clean flavors of the river catch.

= Sticky rice cakes and budbud: Rice cakes wrapped in banana leaves — commonly called budbud or supas in parts of Mindanao — are must-try treats. They show up in breakfast stalls and festivals alike, often enjoyed with coffee or hot chocolate.

- Adobong sahang and shellfish specialties: In rivers and freshwater bodies, small shellfish are harvested and turned into flavorful, bell-pepper and vinegar-forward stews (adobo-style). Local food blogs and guides note these smaller, locally cherished preparations among must-try items for visitors.
